Trialwise univariate analysis

Once you've extracted your parameter estimates, you are ready to start running univariate analyses. The logic is identical to standard univariate contrasts. Imagine you are working with study phase data, and you are trying to figure out whether your region of interest (ROI) exhibits a subsequent memory effect (SME; was this trial remembered or forgotten?). To operationalize an "SME index", you will need to compute the ROI's average across-trial beta for encoding trials which went on to be subsequently remembered, minus the average beta for the trials which went on to be forgotten (akin to running a "remember > forgotten" contrast).

Of course, the main difference between the typical approach (sometimes called "least-squares-unitary"; LSU) and the LSA/LSS is that you obtain betas for each individual trial. Therefore, unlike the LSU, the trialwise approach allows you to compute the trial-by-trial variability of parameter estimates in each condition (you cannot do this in the LSU, obviously, because each condition provides only one beta). As a result, you can (and should) divide the difference between A and B by their pooled standard deviation. (Dividing two means by pooled SD sound familiar? That's because it's the Cohen's D, an effect size for the standardized difference between two means).

Scaling the difference by pooled SD is particularly important in aging studies. Changes in cerebrovascular structure decrease the elasticity of blood vessels, which means that the range of vascular reactivity decreases in older age. Consequently, because fMRI measures blood flow, obtaining a smaller difference between conditions A and B may not actually mean that the difference in neuronal firing is smaller. Thanks to the scaling function, the "SME index" mentioned above becomes insensitive to individual or group differences in the gain of the hemodynamic response function which mediates the neural and fMRI BOLD activity. (However, while the scaling function accounts for age differences in the gain of the HRF, it does not account for potential differences in the variability or shape of the HRF.)
